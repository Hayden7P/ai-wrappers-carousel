Dependency economics 5 / 12

The Black Mirror effect, on frontier platforms.

Ref: "Common People" (S7E1). Caveat: this dynamic applies where you've chosen a single frontier lab. Multi-model and open-weight deployments have materially lower dependency.

01
You sign up. You agreed to the terms.
Like the couple in Common People signing up for a service that seems reasonable at first — the basic tier works, the value is real, the terms are buried. You integrated the model without reading the pricing footnotes.
↳ The value is real. The contract is buried.
02
You can't opt out. Your workflows already depend on it.
Canceling the subscription isn't an option anymore — too much depends on it. Every agent, prompt chain, and team process runs through the platform. Switching means rebuilding from zero.
↳ Opting out costs more than staying in.
03
Each upgrade raises the floor, and your dependency.
Each plan upgrade just maintains the same outcome — you pay more to stay in place. Models improve, limits tighten, costs rise with every new flagship. You can't go back. The escalator only goes up.
↳ Not a trap. A ratchet. Each step is rational. The direction is fixed.

Moat analysis 10 / 12

Two real moats. Two leads dressed up as moats.

Being honest about which is which is the whole investment question.

Moat · durable
Regulated vertical workflow
Proprietary data + compliance + accountable outcomes. A horizontal platform can't ship HIPAA/FINRA/bar-admissible workflows as a feature without years of domain investment.
→ Harvey, Abridge, Hippocratic, Sierra
Moat · durable
Deployment-model choice
Self-hosted, open-source, or on-prem. Addresses a buyer frontier labs structurally can't serve (privacy, air-gap, data residency). Narrow but defensible.
→ n8n, open-weight stacks
Lead · 12–24 mo
Developer context lock-in
Codebase indexing + UX polish buys time, not permanence. Windsurf's carve-up showed IDEs are acquirable. Churn follows the better tool.
→ Cursor, Replit (contested)
Lead · fragile
Own-model differentiation
Frontier training now costs $10B+/year. Subscale labs without a distribution advantage race a cost curve they can't win. Useful as an exit narrative, rarely as a standalone business.
→ Poolside, most "vertical model" plays

Investor thesis 12 / 12

This is not SaaS vs. agentic.
It's about which layer you own.

01
Capital isn't the signal. $14B+ priced VC conviction. The next 24 months price outcomes. Money raised doesn't equal value kept.
02
The supply chain is collapsing into the platform. Claude Design followed the leak of an internal Lovable-equivalent. Cowork was largely built by Claude itself. Suppliers now make in-house what they used to buy.
03
Durable alpha is narrow. Regulated workflows, proprietary data, enterprise contracts, deployment models frontier labs can't serve. Everything else is a lead with a timer.
04
The wrapper era is stratifying, not ending. A few become platforms. More become features. Most become nothing. Knowing which tier you're in is the whole game.
Anthropic isn't replacing startups. It's forcing every AI company to articulate what they actually own, and the ones that can answer honestly will be worth far more for having done so.
